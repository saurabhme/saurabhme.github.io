## Introduction

In the landscape of stochastic optimization, few algorithms are as fundamental
as REINFORCE and Evolutionary Strategies (ES). Often treated as distinct—one the
hammer of Reinforcement Learning, the other a gradient-free optimizer for
"black-box" functions—they are, at a fundamental level, manifestations of the
same principle.

For a physicist, this connection is intuitive: it is the relationship between
microscopic thermal fluctuations and macroscopic drift. Today, we will derive
the ES gradient estimator through two distinct lenses: first as a rigorous
application of REINFORCE, and second via local perturbation analysis (Taylor
expansion).

## 1. The REINFORCE Gradient: A Primer

Let us first rigorously define the REINFORCE gradient (Williams, 1992). Consider
a generic maximization problem where we wish to maximize an expectation over a
distribution parameterized by $\theta$:

$$
J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_\theta(x)} [R(x)] = \int p_\theta(x) R(x) dx
$$

where $R(x)$ is a reward or score function (which need not be differentiable),
and $p_\theta(x)$ is a probability density function.

To optimize $J(\theta)$, we seek the gradient $\nabla_\theta J(\theta)$.
Assuming we can exchange the derivative and the integral (Leibniz integral
rule), we proceed as follows:

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \nabla_\theta \int p_\theta(x) R(x) dx = \int \nabla_\theta p_\theta(x) R(x) dx
$$

Using the "log-derivative trick" (essentially the identity
$\nabla_\theta p = p \nabla_\theta \log p$):

$$
\nabla_\theta p_\theta(x) = p_\theta(x) \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x)
$$

Substituting this back into the integral, we recover the standard REINFORCE
estimator:

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \int p_\theta(x) \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x) R(x) dx = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_\theta} [R(x) \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x)]
$$

This result is powerful because it requires only the score function $R(x)$ and
the score of the density $\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x)$, avoiding the need for
$\nabla_x R(x)$.

## 2. The Variational Perspective: ES is REINFORCE

Now, let us see how Evolutionary Strategies is simply a special case of the
framework above. In ES, we do not optimize $F(\theta)$ directly. Instead, we
optimize a "Gaussian-smoothed" objective:

$$
J_{ES}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\psi \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, \sigma^2 I)} [F(\psi)]
$$

Here, $\psi$ is a perturbed parameter vector and $F(\psi)$ is the "hard"
objective. To derive the gradient, we map the components to the REINFORCE
framework:

- The "Action" $x$ becomes the parameters $\psi$.

- The "Policy" $p_\theta(x)$ becomes the search distribution
  $p(\psi | \theta) = \mathcal{N}(\psi; \theta, \sigma^2 I)$.

- The "Reward" $R(x)$ becomes the function value $F(\psi)$.

Applying the REINFORCE identity:

$$
\nabla_\theta J_{ES}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\psi \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, \sigma^2 I)} [F(\psi) \nabla_\theta \log p(\psi | \theta)]
$$

The log-likelihood of the Gaussian is
$\log p \propto -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \|\psi - \theta\|^2$. Its gradient with
respect to the mean $\theta$ is:

$$
\nabla_\theta \log p(\psi | \theta) = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} (\psi - \theta)
$$

Using the reparameterization trick $\psi = \theta + \epsilon$ where
$\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)$, we arrive at the standard ES
estimator:

$$
\nabla_\theta J_{ES}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} \left[ F(\theta + \epsilon) \frac{\epsilon}{\sigma^2} \right]
$$

This derivation confirms that ES is simply REINFORCE where the "policy" is the
parameter generator itself.

## 3. The Perturbation Perspective: A Taylor Expansion

While the REINFORCE derivation is elegant, it can feel abstract. A more
mechanical justification—one that highlights _why_ noise estimates a
gradient—comes from a simple Taylor expansion.

Consider a 1D function $F(\theta)$ (the logic holds for multivariate). We
perturb $\theta$ by noise $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$. We can
approximate $F$ locally:

$$
F(\theta + \epsilon) \approx F(\theta) + F'(\theta)\epsilon + \frac{1}{2}F''(\theta)\epsilon^2
$$

Now, consider the expectation of the product of the noise $\epsilon$ and the
perturbed function value (the numerator of our ES estimator):

$$
\mathbb{E}[\epsilon F(\theta + \epsilon)] \approx \mathbb{E}\left[ \epsilon \left( F(\theta) + F'(\theta)\epsilon + \frac{1}{2}F''(\theta)\epsilon^2 \right) \right]
$$

By linearity of expectation, we analyze term by term:

1. $\mathbb{E}[\epsilon F(\theta)] = F(\theta)\mathbb{E}[\epsilon] = 0$ (since
   mean is 0)

2. $\mathbb{E}[\epsilon (F'(\theta)\epsilon)] = F'(\theta)\mathbb{E}[\epsilon^2] = F'(\theta)\sigma^2$

3. $\mathbb{E}[\epsilon (\frac{1}{2}F''(\theta)\epsilon^2)] = \frac{1}{2}F''(\theta)\mathbb{E}[\epsilon^3] = 0$
   (skewness of Gaussian is 0)

Thus, we are left with:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\epsilon F(\theta + \epsilon)] \approx F'(\theta)\sigma^2 \implies F'(\theta) \approx \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\mathbb{E}[\epsilon F(\theta + \epsilon)]
$$

This recovers the exact same estimator as the REINFORCE derivation! This
perspective is powerful because it reveals that **ES is essentially a stochastic
finite-difference method** that uses the variance of the noise ($\sigma^2$) to
probe the slope ($F'$).

## 4. The "Free Lunch": Second-Order Information

One of the most compelling insights from the perturbation view is that we are
not limited to the gradient. Since we have already evaluated
$F(\theta+\epsilon)$, can we extract curvature (Hessian) information without
extra function calls?

Revisiting our Taylor expansion, look at the expectation of
$(\epsilon^2 - \sigma^2) F(\theta + \epsilon)$. The first-order term vanishes
because $\mathbb{E}[\epsilon^3]=0$. The second-order term, however, involves
$\mathbb{E}[\epsilon^4]$ (the kurtosis).

For a Gaussian, $\mathbb{E}[\epsilon^4] = 3\sigma^4$. It can be shown that:

$$
\mathbb{E}\left[ \left(\frac{\epsilon^2}{\sigma^2} - 1\right) F(\theta + \epsilon) \right] \approx \sigma^2 F''(\theta)
$$

This implies we can estimate the diagonal of the Hessian **using the exact same
samples** used for the gradient. In physics terms, if the gradient is the dipole
moment of the perturbation, the Hessian is the quadrupole moment.

## Conclusion

We have bridged two worlds:

1. **Probabilistic:** ES is REINFORCE applied to a Gaussian policy over
   parameters.

2. **Deterministic:** ES is a smoothed finite-difference approximation via
   Taylor expansion.

This duality explains its robustness. By smoothing the landscape (convolving $F$
with a Gaussian), ES trades the bias of not optimizing $F$ directly for the
ability to ignore high-frequency noise and non-differentiable singularities. It
turns the chaos of high-dimensional optimization into an ordered, smooth
descent.

# Appendix: The Geometry of Gaussian Evolution

## 1. The Fisher Information Matrix (FIM)

In Information Geometry, the parameter space of a probability distribution is
treated as a Riemannian manifold. The metric tensor for this manifold is the
Fisher Information Matrix $F(\theta)$:

$$
F(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_\theta} \left[ \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x) \left( \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x) \right)^T \right]
$$

This matrix measures the local curvature of the KL-divergence. Steepest ascent
in this Riemannian space is given by the **Natural Gradient**:

$$
\tilde{\nabla}_\theta J = F(\theta)^{-1} \nabla_\theta J
$$

## 2. Derivation for the Multivariate Gaussian

Let our policy be $p_\theta(x) = \mathcal{N}(x | \mu, \Sigma)$. The parameters
are $\theta = [\mu, \Sigma]$. The log-likelihood is:

$$
\log p_\theta(x) = -\frac{d}{2}\log(2\pi) - \frac{1}{2}\log|\Sigma| - \frac{1}{2}(x - \mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (x - \mu)
$$

### Part A: The Mean Parameter $\mu$

First, we find the score function with respect to the mean $\mu$:

$$
\nabla_\mu \log p_\theta(x) = \Sigma^{-1}(x - \mu)
$$

Now, we calculate the Fisher Information block corresponding to $\mu$, denoted
$F_\mu$:

$$
F_\mu = \mathbb{E} \left[ \left( \Sigma^{-1}(x - \mu) \right) \left( \Sigma^{-1}(x - \mu) \right)^T \right]
$$

$$
F_\mu = \Sigma^{-1} \mathbb{E} \left[ (x - \mu)(x - \mu)^T \right] \Sigma^{-T}
$$

Since $\mathbb{E} [(x - \mu)(x - \mu)^T]$ is exactly the definition of the
covariance $\Sigma$:

$$
F_\mu = \Sigma^{-1} \Sigma \Sigma^{-1} = \Sigma^{-1}
$$

**The Natural Gradient Update for** $\mu$**:**

$$
\tilde{\nabla}_\mu J = F_\mu^{-1} \nabla_\mu J = \Sigma \nabla_\mu J
$$

This is a profound result. In standard Euclidean gradient descent, the update is
$\mu \leftarrow \mu + \alpha \nabla_\mu J$. In Natural Evolution Strategies, the
update becomes $\mu \leftarrow \mu + \alpha \Sigma \nabla_\mu J$.

### Part B: Connection to the Hessian

Why does multiplying by $\Sigma$ resemble second-order optimization?

Consider a quadratic objective function (a local Taylor expansion of our fitness
landscape) near the optimum:

$$
f(x) \approx -\frac{1}{2} (x - x^*)^T H (x - x^*)
$$

where $H$ is the Hessian (positive definite for a maximum).

In this landscape, the optimal search distribution (the one maximizing average
fitness entropy-regularized) will align its covariance with the shape of the
peak. specifically, the optimal covariance satisfies $\Sigma \propto H^{-1}$.

If the algorithm effectively adapts $\Sigma$ to match the inverse Hessian of the
landscape (which NES does), then the update for the mean becomes:

$$
\mu_{new} \leftarrow \mu + \alpha \Sigma \nabla_\mu J \approx \mu + \alpha H^{-1} \nabla_\mu J
$$

This recovers **Newton's Method**.

Therefore, by doing steepest ascent on the statistical manifold (changing $\mu$
and $\Sigma$), Natural ES implicitly performs a quasi-Newton optimization on the
objective function $f(x)$. The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ acts as a learned
preconditioner, dynamically estimating the inverse Hessian $H^{-1}$ from the
history of function evaluations.
