## Introduction

The paper _"Back to Basics: Let Denoising Generative Models Denoise"_

<Cite id={1} /> argues that predicting clean data ($x$) is fundamentally superior
to predicting noise ($\epsilon$) or velocity ($v$) due to manifold assumptions. They
propose "Just Image Transformers" (JiT) operating directly on pixels/patches.

We assert that this formulation is not a distinct paradigm but rather a specific
instantiation of the **Latent Stochastic Interpolants (LSI)** framework

<Cite id={2} />, where the latent mapping is restricted to a deterministic
identity. By deriving the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) under these constraints,
we show that $x$, $v$, and $\epsilon$ prediction are merely reparameterizations
of the same likelihood bound, and the empirical differences arise from implicit
loss weighting rather than manifold alignment.

## Unifying Formulation

We adopt the notation from _LSI_:

- Let $x_1$ denote the observed data (pixels).
- Let $z_1$ denote the latent representation at time $t=1$.
- Let $z_0$ denote the prior at time $t=0$, assumed to be Gaussian
  $z_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ (matching the BtB assumption).
- Let $z_t$ be the stochastic interpolant between $z_0$ and $z_1$.

### The LSI Evidence Lower Bound

The core contribution of LSI is the derivation of an ELBO for dynamical systems
governed by stochastic interpolants. From **Eq. (3)** in _lsi.pdf_, the ELBO is
given by:

$$
\ln p_\theta(x_1) \ge \mathbb{E}_{q(z_1|x_1)} \left[ \ln p_\theta(x_1|z_1) - \mathbb{E}_{z_t} \left[ \int_0^1 \frac{1}{2} ||u(z_t, t)||^2 dt \right] \right]
$$

Here, $u(z_t, t)$ is the vector field that satisfies the matching condition
$\sigma_t u(z_t, t) = h_\phi(z_t, t) - h_\theta(z_t, t)$, where $h_\phi$ is the
drift of the variational posterior and $h_\theta$ is the learnable model drift.

### Collapsing the Latent Space (The BtB Assumption)

The _Back to Basics_ paper operates directly on patches of pixels without a
learned VAE. In the LSI framework, this is equivalent to defining the encoder
$q(z_1|x_1)$ and decoder $p_\theta(x_1|z_1)$ as deterministic, invertible
mappings (e.g., identity or patch flattening).

Let us define:

$$
q(z_1|x_1) = \delta(z_1 - x_1)
$$

$$
p_\theta(x_1|z_1) = \delta(x_1 - z_1)
$$

This implies $z_1 \equiv x_1$.

### Deriving the Deterministic Bound

Substituting these Dirac distributions into the ELBO:

1. **The Reconstruction Term**: The term
   $\mathbb{E}_{q(z_1|x_1)} [\ln p_\theta(x_1|z_1)]$ becomes constant (or
   infinite but constant with respect to generative parameters) because the
   reconstruction is perfect and deterministic. It can be ignored during
   optimization of the generative model.

2. **The Generative Term**: With $z_1 = x_1$, the ELBO simplifies to:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{ELBO} \approx - \mathbb{E}_{x_1, z_0, t} \left[ \frac{1}{2} ||u(z_t, t)||^2 \right] + \text{const}
$$

We assume the linear stochastic interpolant used in both papers
($\kappa_t=t, \nu_t=1-t$). From **Eq. (15)** in _lsi.pdf_, the target field
$u(z_t, t)$ for this interpolant is:

$$
u(z_t, t) = \sigma^{-1} \left[ -\sigma \sqrt{\frac{t}{1-t}}\epsilon + z_1 - z_0 - h_\theta(z_t, t) \right]
$$

Substituting $z_1 = x$ (clean data) and $z_0 = \epsilon$ (noise), we obtain the
general LSI loss for the deterministic model:

$$
\mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \left|\left| -\sigma \sqrt{\frac{t}{1-t}}\epsilon + x - \epsilon - h_\theta(z_t, t) \right|\right|^2 \right]
$$

## Equivalence of Prediction Objectives

The _Back to Basics_ paper posits that predicting $x$ (clean data) is
fundamentally distinct from predicting $\epsilon$ (noise) or $v$ (velocity).
However, using the LSI ELBO derived above, we demonstrate that these are merely
algebraic reparameterizations of the **same** optimization objective, differing
only by a time-dependent weighting coefficient.

### $x$-prediction (Denoising)

The "denoising" objective favored by BtB corresponds to the **Denoising
Parameterization** in LSI (Appendix A.3).

We parameterize the drift $h_\theta$ in terms of a clean data predictor network
$\hat{x}_\theta(z_t, t)$ using the relationship
$h_\theta(z_t, t) = \frac{\hat{x}_\theta(z_t, t) - z_t}{1-t}$.

Substituting this into the LSI objective (specifically **Eq. 39** in _lsi.pdf_),
the objective becomes:

$$
\mathcal{L}_x = \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{1}{2(1-t)^2} || x - \hat{x}_\theta(z_t, t) ||^2 \right]
$$

This reveals that $x$-prediction is simply optimizing the ELBO with a weighting
schedule of $\lambda_t = \frac{1}{2(1-t)^2}$. The LSI framework confirms this is
a valid maximization of the likelihood bound.

### $\epsilon$-prediction (Noise Prediction)

The standard diffusion objective corresponds to the **NoisePred
Parameterization** in LSI (Appendix A.4).

We parameterize the drift in terms of a noise predictor network
$\hat{\epsilon}_\theta(z_t, t)$. Following the exact derivation in **Eq. (46)**
of _lsi.pdf_, the objective takes the form:

$$
\mathcal{L}_\epsilon = \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{\sigma^2 t + 1 - t}{2 t^2(1-t)} || \epsilon - \hat{\epsilon}_\theta(z_t, t) ||^2 \right]
$$

Here, the implicit weighting schedule behaves approximately as
$\lambda_t \propto t^{-2}$ near $t=0$ and $\lambda_t \propto (1-t)^{-1}$ near
$t=1$. This recovers the standard noise-prediction loss, showing it optimizes
the same bound but with heavy emphasis on the noise end of the schedule
($t \to 0$).

### $v$-prediction (Velocity)

The velocity prediction objective corresponds to the **OrigFlow**
parameterization in LSI (Appendix A.1).

Using the derivation in **Eq. (24)** of _lsi.pdf_, the objective is:

$$
\mathcal{L}_v = \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{1}{2\sigma^2(1-t)} || \underbrace{\sqrt{1-t}(x - \epsilon) - \sigma\sqrt{t}\epsilon}_{\text{Target}(z_t)} - \hat{v}_\theta(z_t, t) ||^2 \right]
$$

Here, the network $\hat{v}_\theta$ predicts a target that is dominated by the
velocity term $\sqrt{1-t}(x - \epsilon)$. Crucially, the weighting coefficient
is $\lambda_t = \frac{1}{2\sigma^2(1-t)}$. This demonstrates that stochastic
velocity prediction essentially optimizes the ELBO with a weighting of
$\propto (1-t)^{-1}$. This places higher emphasis on the data end of the
trajectory ($t \to 1$) compared to the noise end, similar to $x$-prediction but
with a lower-order pole ($(1-t)^{-1}$ vs $(1-t)^{-2}$).

## Conclusion

The "Just Image Transformers" (JiT) approach proposed in _Back to Basics_ is
effectively a special case of **Latent Stochastic Interpolants (LSI)** where the
encoder and decoder are set to identity functions.

Our derivation proves that the objectives for predicting clean data ($x$), noise
($\epsilon$), and velocity ($v$) all originate from the **same Evidence Lower
Bound**. The performance gap observed in _Back to Basics_ is not due to manifold
fundamentals, but rather due to the specific implicit weightings and
singularities imposed by the ELBO:

- **x-pred:** Weight has a pole at $t=1$ ($\propto (1-t)^{-2}$).
- **$\epsilon$-pred:** Weight has a pole at $t=0$ ($\propto t^{-2}$).
- **v-pred:** Weight has a pole at $t=1$ ($\propto (1-t)^{-1}$).

LSI provides the theoretical unification that explains why $x$-prediction
performs best: it aligns the optimization focus (via the stronger weighting
pole) with the region where the signal is strongest ($t \to 1$), whereas
$v$-prediction uses a weaker weighting for that same region.
