## Introduction

[Rectified Flow](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.02747) is a generative modelling
framework that learns to transport a source distribution to a target
distribution. Often, I see it claimed that the trajectories of the learned flow
are straight(er). This is not true in general. In fact, original paper only
suggests this for $k$-Rectified Flows, with $k > 1$. The $1$-Rectified Flow
case, which is the most common case, does not have this property. The
$1$-Rectified Flow models are actually the same as other Flow and Diffusion
models and therefore there is no reason to expect that the trajectories would be
straighter.

Let's first review what rectified flows are and then we will see why the
trajectories are not straight and what we can do about that.

## Notation

We use small Latin letters $t, x, y$ etc. to represent scalar and vector
variables, $f, g$ etc. to represent functions, Greek letters $\alpha, \beta$
etc. to represent (hyper-)parameters, and capital letters $G$ to represent
matrices. With a slight abuse of notation we use lower case letters $x$ to
represent both the random variable and a particular value $x \sim p(x)$.
Whenever unambiguous, we suppress the dependence of state $x_t$ on time $t$ as
$x \equiv x_t$, and dependence of functions on state $x_t$ and time $t$ as
$f \equiv f(x_t, t)$ to simplify notation.

## $k$-Rectified Flows

Rectified Flow (Liu et al., 2022) is a simple yet powerful framework that
constructs an ordinary differential equation (ODE) model to transport a
distribution $p_0$ to $p_1$.

The core idea starts with a simple linear interpolation between samples from the
source and target distributions. Let $x_0 \sim p_0$ and $x_1 \sim p_1$. We
define an interpolation process:

$$
x_t = t x_1 + (1-t) x_0, \quad t \in [0, 1]
$$

The velocity of this individual trajectory is simply the constant vector
$x_1 - x_0$. However, we want a _globally_ defined vector field $v(x, t)$ that
can generate these marginals. The Rectified Flow objective seeks the vector
field that best matches these individual straight-line velocities in the
least-squares sense:

$$
\min_v \int_0^1 \mathbb{E}_{x_t} [ \| v(x_t, t) - (x_1 - x_0) \|^2 ] dt
$$

This is the **1-Rectified Flow**.

While the interpolation paths $x_t$ are straight lines by construction, the
trajectories generated by simulating the learned ODE $dx/dt = v(x, t)$ are **not
necessarily straight**. The vector field $v(x, t)$ is an average over all
possible $(x_0, x_1)$ pairs that could pass through $x$ at time $t$. If multiple
straight paths cross at the same point, the ODE trajectory will follow the
average direction, potentially curving.

To fix this crossing problem and straighten the paths, the authors introduce a
recursive procedure called **Reflow**.

1. Generate pairs $(x_0, x_1^1)$ by simulating the 1-Rectified Flow starting
   from $x_0$.
2. Train a new Rectified Flow on these new pairs $(x_0, x_1^1)$.

This yields a **2-Rectified Flow**. Repeating this process $k$ times yields a
**$k$-Rectified Flow**. As $k \to \infty$, the trajectories are guaranteed to
become non-crossing straight lines, minimizing the transport cost.

## $1$-Rectified Flows are special case of Flow models

Both Flow Matching (Lipman et al., 2023) and Rectified Flows propose to regress
a vector field $v(x_t, t)$ to a target vector field $u_t(x_t)$ that generates a
probability path $p_t(x)$. The general Flow Matching objective is:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{FM} = \mathbb{E}_{t, x_t \sim p_t(x)} \| v(x_t, t) - u_t(x_t) \|^2
$$

Since the marginal vector field $u_t(x)$ is generally intractable, Lipman et al.
introduced **Conditional Flow Matching (CFM)**. Instead of matching the marginal
field directly, we match the vector field of a conditional probability path
$p_t(x|z)$ conditioned on a latent variable $z$. The CFM objective is:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{CFM} = \mathbb{E}_{t, q(z), x_t \sim p_t(x|z)} \| v(x_t, t) - u_t(x_t|z) \|^2
$$

If we choose the conditioning variable $z$ to be the data pair $(x_0, x_1)$, and
define the conditional flow as the linear interpolation
$x_t = t x_1 + (1-t) x_0$, then the conditional vector field is simply
$u_t(x_t|x_0, x_1) = x_1 - x_0$. Substituting this into the CFM objective:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{RF} = \mathbb{E}_{t, p(x_0, x_1), x_t} \| v(x_t, t) - (x_1 - x_0) \|^2
$$

This recovers exactly the Rectified Flow objective.

Tong et al. (2023) further generalized this in "Improving and Generalizing
Flow-Based Generative Models with Minibatch Optimal Transport". They observed
that while the marginal flows are equivalent, the choice of coupling
$p(x_0, x_1)$ matters for training efficiency. Independent coupling (random
pairs) corresponds to standard CFM/RF, while Optimal Transport couplings can
lead to straighter paths and faster training—a concept closely related to the
"Reflow" procedure in Rectified Flows.

## Two Gaussian Special Case for Rectified Flow

We consider the case where both source and target are Gaussian distributions
with the same variance $\sigma^2 I$ but different means. Let
$p_0 = \mathcal{N}(x; \mu_0, \sigma^2 I)$ and
$p_1 = \mathcal{N}(x; \mu_1, \sigma^2 I)$. We assume the coupling is
independent, meaning $p(x_0, x_1) = p_0(x_0) p_1(x_1)$.

The interpolation state is $x_t = (1-t)x_0 + t x_1$. Since $x_0$ and $x_1$ are
independent Gaussians, $x_t$ is also Gaussian:

$$
p_t(x_t) = \mathcal{N}(x_t; \mu_t, \sigma_t^2 I)
$$

where the mean evolves linearly as $\mu_t = (1-t)\mu_0 + t\mu_1$, but the
variance evolves quadratically:

$$
\sigma_t^2 = (1-t)^2 \sigma^2 + t^2 \sigma^2 = (1 - 2t + 2t^2) \sigma^2
$$

Notice that the variance **dips** in the middle at $t=0.5$, reducing to
$0.5\sigma^2$. The distribution contracts and then expands.

The optimal vector field $v(x, t)$ for a 1-Rectified Flow is given by the
conditional expectation $v(x,t) = \mathbb{E}[x_1 - x_0 | x_t = x]$. For Gaussian
variables, this expectation is linear in $x$. Using standard properties of
conditional Gaussians (see Singh et al., 2022), we can derive:

$$
v(x, t) = (\mu_1 - \mu_0) + \frac{2t - 1}{1 - 2t + 2t^2} (x - \mu_t)
$$

The first term $(\mu_1 - \mu_0)$ is the constant velocity of the mean. The
second term is a time-dependent linear scaling that pushes particles towards the
mean when $t < 0.5$ (contraction) and away from the mean when $t > 0.5$
(expansion).

Because of this second term, any particle not exactly on the line connecting the
means will follow a **curved trajectory**.

## Straight Trajectories for the Two Gaussian Special Case

As we saw, the curvature arises from the time-dependent interpolation of the
variance $\sigma_t^2$. For the trajectories to be straight in general, the
vector field should not have the "breathing" term that depends on particle
position $x$. This requires the coefficient of $(x - \mu_t)$ in the vector field
to be zero.

Let's assume a general schedule $x_t = \alpha_t x_1 + \beta_t x_0$, effectively
interpolating the means as $\mu_t = \alpha_t \mu_1 + \beta_t \mu_0$. The
variance of the interpolant is:

$$
\sigma_t^2 = (\alpha_t^2 + \beta_t^2) \sigma^2
$$

The vector field for this general schedule (again derived via conditional
expectation for Gaussians) is:

$$
v(x, t) = \dot{\mu}_t + \frac{\dot{\sigma}_t}{\sigma_t} (x - \mu_t)
$$

For the trajectories to be straight lines parallel to the mean shift, we need
the second term to vanish for all $x$. This implies $\dot{\sigma}_t = 0$,
meaning the variance must be constant in time:

$$
\sigma_t^2 = (\alpha_t^2 + \beta_t^2) \sigma^2 = \text{const} = \sigma^2
$$

Thus, we require the schedule coefficients to lie on the unit circle:

$$
\alpha_t^2 + \beta_t^2 = 1
$$

This is satisfied by the trigonometric (or Variance Preserving) schedule widely
used in Diffusion Models:

$$
\alpha_t = \sin(\frac{\pi t}{2}), \quad \beta_t = \cos(\frac{\pi t}{2})
$$

Under this schedule, $\sigma_t^2 = \sigma^2$ for all $t$, so
$\dot{\sigma}_t = 0$. The vector field simplifies to $v(x, t) = \dot{\mu}_t$,
which depends only on time. The particles move in unison, translating the
distribution without expanding or contracting it, resulting in perfectly
straight, parallel trajectories.

This highlights a key insight: **Standard linear interpolation results in curved
Rectified Flow paths because it fails to preserve the geometry (variance) of the
independent coupling.** Preserving the variance via a spherical interpolation
rectifies the flow 1-step.

## Straight Trajectories with Different Variances

What if the variances are different, i.e.,
$p_0 = \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2 I)$ and
$p_1 = \mathcal{N}(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2 I)$ with $\sigma_0 \neq \sigma_1$?

We can derive the conditions for straight trajectories more rigorously using the
general form of the optimal vector field. As shown in Appendix F of _Singh et
al. (2022)_, the vector field for a Gaussian probability path
$p_t(x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu_t, \sigma_t^2 I)$ is given by:

$$
v_t(x) = \dot{\mu}_t + \frac{\dot{\sigma}_t}{\sigma_t} (x - \mu_t)
$$

For the trajectories to be straight lines, the velocity of any particle must be
constant in time. Let's analyze the velocity of a particle $x_t$. We can write
$x_t$ using the reparameterization trick as $x_t = \mu_t + \sigma_t \epsilon$,
where $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ is fixed for the particle. Substituting
this into the velocity field:

$$
v_t(x_t) = \dot{\mu}_t + \frac{\dot{\sigma}_t}{\sigma_t} (\mu_t + \sigma_t \epsilon - \mu_t) = \dot{\mu}_t + \dot{\sigma}_t \epsilon
$$

For the trajectory $x_t$ to be a straight line, this velocity $v_t(x_t)$ must be
constant for all $t$. Since this must hold for _any_ particle (any $\epsilon$),
both terms must be constant independently:

1.  $\dot{\mu}_t = \text{const} \implies \mu_t$ is linear in $t$.
2.  $\dot{\sigma}_t = \text{const} \implies \sigma_t$ is linear in $t$.

The standard linear interpolation $x_t = (1-t)x_0 + t x_1$ satisfies the first
condition (linear mean) but fails the second. As we saw, the variance
$\sigma_t^2 = (1-t)^2 \sigma_0^2 + t^2 \sigma_1^2$ is convex, meaning $\sigma_t$
is not linear (unless $\sigma_0 = \sigma_1$).

To achieve straight trajectories, we essentially need the particles to move at
constant velocity. For Gaussian distributions, this implies that both the mean
$\mu_t$ and the standard deviation $\sigma_t$ must evolve linearly in time:

$$
\mu_t = (1-t)\mu_0 + t\mu_1
$$

$$
\sigma_t = (1-t)\sigma_0 + t\sigma_1
$$

Can we achieve this with a schedule of the form
$x_t = \alpha_t x_1 + \beta_t x_0$?

The conditions on the schedule become:

1. $\alpha_t \mu_1 + \beta_t \mu_0 = \mu_t$
2. $\alpha_t^2 \sigma_1^2 + \beta_t^2 \sigma_0^2 = \sigma_t^2$

If $\mu_0$ and $\mu_1$ are linearly independent (which is the case if the origin
does not lie on the line connecting them), the first equation is a vector
equation that uniquely determines the coefficients: it forces $\alpha_t = t$ and
$\beta_t = 1-t$. This leaves no degrees of freedom to satisfy the second
equation (variance), which, as we saw, fails for the linear schedule.

**Why does it depend on the coordinate system?** The interpolation formula
$x_t = \alpha_t x_1 + \beta_t x_0$ is not affine-invariant unless
$\alpha_t + \beta_t = 1$. When we relax this condition to satisfy the variance
constraint, the specific values of $x_0$ and $x_1$ (and thus the origin
location) matter.

However, if we choose a coordinate system where the origin lies on the line
passing through $\mu_0$ and $\mu_1$ (making them collinear/dependent), the
vector constraint on the mean degrades into a single scalar constraint. This
provides an extra degree of freedom, allowing us to find a non-linear schedule
$(\alpha_t, \beta_t)$ that satisfies both:

1.  The mean moves linearly along the line.
2.  The variance evolves linearly
    ($\alpha_t^2 \sigma_1^2 + \beta_t^2 \sigma_0^2 = ((1-t)\sigma_0 + t\sigma_1)^2$).

Thus, straight trajectories for independent couplings with different variances
are possible, but they require a constructive schedue defined relative to the
specific geometry of the means.
